Clock 1 Prototype — Runner Orbit Clock

The runner goes around a circular track once every hour. The angle shows how far the runner is into the current hour (minute + seconds). The runner dot shifts from cool to hot pink/purple as the minute gets later, like they're getting more fatigued. The background brightness changes with the real hour of day (darker at night, brighter mid-day), so this starts to feel like a “training loop” instead of just a generic clock. The ring also pulses color with each second, and the clock plays a tiny “lap beep” when a new minute starts.

Sketch narrative

  • Context: This clock is for runners who think in terms of pacing and distance, not just wall time. It’s meant to live on a smartwatch face or training app and answer: “How deep am I into this hour?” not just “What time is it?”
  • Design decisions:
    • Position on the circle encodes minute+second, so time literally becomes distance around a loop.
    • The dot color heats up across the hour to communicate effort / fatigue.
    • Background brightness is mapped to hour-of-day to hint at time of day without needing extra UI.
    • The dot movement is smoothed with easing instead of snapping each second, so it feels like steady running.
    • I added a tiny chime per minute based on feedback that runners like an audible “lap beep.”
  • Future work: Add a pause interaction (water break / rest interval), or layer in total miles for the session so each loop can represent not just time, but distance accumulated.

Clock 2 Prototype — Pace Bar Clock (Interactive)

This clock treats each minute like a sprint rep. The teal bar fills left to right over the 60 seconds, so you can see how deep you are into “this rep.” The bar also subtly “breathes” (its height pulses smoothly) to suggest physical effort. Above it is a digital timestamp and a live lap counter. When you click, the bar “jumps lane” and the lap counter increments — based on feedback like “I always tap my watch when I start a new interval, can I log that?”

Sketch narrative

  • Context: This is for interval training. During repeats (like 1 min on / 1 min off), runners care less about wall clock time and more about “how far into this rep am I right now?”
  • Design decisions:
    • The horizontal bar width = seconds within the current minute.
    • The pulsing bar height = breathing/effort.
    • Clicking toggles the bar’s lane position and increments a lap counter, so you can mark each completed rep the same way you’d double tap a watch.
    • Dark navy background + high-contrast teal is meant for glanceability when you’re tired/sweaty.
  • Future work: Persist lap history across multiple minutes, and flash or vibrate when a set ends. Eventually color the bar by effort zone (easy / tempo / all-out).

Clock 3 Prototype — Route Progress Clock (Final)

This clock turns time into distance on a running loop. The polyline is a route (like running around a lake). Your marker moves around it based on the current minute and second. The part of the path you’ve “already run this hour” glows teal. Checkpoint dots at 25%, 50%, 75% light up once you’ve passed them — basically mile markers. The digital time sits nearby like a race clock.

Sketch narrative

  • Context: This is for runners who think in routes, not just numbers. It answers “Where am I on the loop right now?” instead of only “What time is it?”
  • Design decisions:
    • The looped route encodes one hour.
    • Position along the route encodes minute+second.
    • The completed segment glows bright teal, and the rest stays dim — so the current hour looks like a partially finished run.
    • Checkpoints at 25%, 50%, and 75% light up when reached, based on peer feedback: “I want to know when I’ve hit halfway.”
  • Future work: Add hover/click to show split time for each checkpoint, and eventually layer in elevation or heart-rate colors to tell what parts of the loop are hardest.
